# 05 — Audience Profile

## ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)

| Attribute             | Definition                                                                  |
| ---                   | ---                                                                         |
| Company size          | 5–250 employees                                                             |
| Stage                 | Series-A or earlier; bootstrapped consultancies up to $5M ARR               |
| Geography             | English-language operations, time-zone-tolerant (UTC-8 to UTC+5)           |
| Buyer role            | Founder/CEO, Ops Director, Head of Customer Success, COO                     |
| Tech stack            | Notion / Slack / HubSpot / Linear / Drive — at least three of                |
| Existing AI use       | Active ChatGPT Plus or Team subscriber for ≥3 months                         |
| Buying authority      | Can spend $480–$5,800 USD on a vendor without procurement escalation         |
| Buying trigger        | Used a Store GPT for a serious work problem in the past 2 weeks              |

## Persona 1 — Maya (Primary)

**Demographics.** 34, founder + lead consultant at a 12-person research firm in Lisbon. Subscribed to ChatGPT Plus since GPT-4 launch. Engineering degree, never wrote code professionally. Working spouse, no children, modest cat.

**Goals.**
1. Stop being the bottleneck on every pricing/scoping decision.
2. Codify the team's research methodology into reusable artifacts.
3. Hit $1.2M ARR by Q4 without growing the team beyond 15.

**Frustrations.**
1. Discovery calls. She refuses to do them. Will book one only after a paid trial.
2. Agencies that show up with five-person delivery teams to her four-person company.
3. Having to re-explain her business model on every vendor onboarding.

**Channels she lives in.** Notion (daily), Slack (constantly), Twitter/X for industry signal, ChatGPT for late-night research and pricing checks. Avoids LinkedIn except for occasional ICP research. Does not click on email newsletters.

**How she finds us.** Saturday night, late: pastes a pricing question into a Store GPT we publish. The GPT answers competently and ends with our deep link in the close.

**What converts her.** The transcript card on the hero — specifically that the "After" side is signed by a named operator. That naming is the proof for her.

**Buying signal.** She clicks Pay-per-engagement, not Subscription. Tier 1 exists for her.

**Anti-signal.** If she sees a calendar booking widget, she closes the tab.

## Persona 2 — Daniel (Secondary)

**Demographics.** 41, Director of Operations at a 200-person B2B SaaS in Austin. Reports to the COO. Eight years in ops; previous role was at a 700-person company with a real procurement department.

**Goals.**
1. Cut the time his team spends on RFP responses by 40%.
2. Standardize the customer onboarding playbook so it doesn't depend on three senior CSMs.
3. Look like a strategic operator to his COO.

**Frustrations.**
1. Has burned $80k on three previous "AI consultant" engagements. Two showed up with slide decks; one disappeared.
2. His company's AP system doesn't know how to handle vendors under $5k.
3. Internal stakeholders are tired of him recommending tools that don't ship.

**Channels he lives in.** LinkedIn (professional only), Slack (work), Notion (work-shared), an industry-specific Slack community for ops directors. Reads two newsletters weekly.

**How he finds us.** A peer in the ops Slack community shares a screenshot of an output from one of our published GPTs. He tries it himself. It produces a usable artifact.

**What converts him.** The FAQ section, specifically "Is this a tool or a service?" The answer is honest about being both. He values that honesty.

**Buying signal.** He clicks Subscription. The featured saffron border + the Tuesday/Thursday cadence in the bullets reads "this is real infrastructure, not theater."

**Anti-signal.** Marketing language like "supercharge your ops" or "AI-powered." He closes the tab.

## Persona 3 — Lin (Champion / influencer, not buyer)

**Demographics.** 29, brand-side ops generalist at a 40-person agency in Toronto. Reports to the agency's CEO. Three years in agency ops, one year using ChatGPT seriously.

**Goals.** (Personal — different from buyer goals.)
1. Be seen as the person who brings tooling that actually works.
2. Earn back two hours a day from copy-paste runbook work.
3. Make a case for promotion via a measurable cost-savings story.

**Why she matters.** She's not the buyer. She's the *internal champion* who finds the GPT, runs it through real work, then shows the output to the CEO.

**Channels she lives in.** Twitter/X, ChatGPT, a Discord for agency ops generalists, internal Slack at her firm.

**How she finds us.** Saw a tweet referencing one of our Store GPTs. Tried it. Got a usable runbook in 20 minutes. Took a screenshot.

**What converts the buyer behind her.** The reference call we offer. Lin's screenshot got the buyer interested; the reference call gets them to sign.

**Buying signal.** Buyer (CEO) clicks Concierge after the reference call.

**Anti-signal.** No public case studies on the page. The CEO interpreted the absence as disciplined, not as missing.

## Anti-personas

We are wrong for these visitors. Visual hierarchy, copy, and pricing are calibrated to gently filter them out.

### A1 — The bargain hunter

Wants the GPT for free, never the engagement. Will read the Store GPT, leave, and never return. We accept the bounce.

### A2 — The category-curious AI tourist

Wants to "see what AI agencies are doing now." Won't pay; won't engage. They are not in our funnel.

### A3 — The enterprise procurement-led buyer

Wants W-9, NDA, vendor security questionnaire, 60-day net invoice. We are too small. We tell them so, kindly, in the FAQ-adjacent reply when they email.

### A4 — The pure-automation seeker

Wants a tool that runs entirely without human oversight. We are high-touch. They should buy n8n or Zapier.

### A5 — The "build me a custom GPT" buyer

Wants us to *build them* a GPT they own and publish. This is a different service line (one-off custom-GPT builds for $8k+) and is out of scope for After-the-Store. We refer these to a separate intake.

## Distribution by tier (assumed)

Of every 100 paid customers, we expect roughly:

- 60 Pay-per-engagement (Maya pattern)
- 30 Subscription (Daniel pattern)
- 10 Concierge (Lin's CEO pattern, plus a few direct enterprise inbound)

This skew informs the visual weight of each tier — Subscription is featured because it's the highest LTV non-anomaly path, but Pay-per-engagement is listed first because it's the volume entry.
